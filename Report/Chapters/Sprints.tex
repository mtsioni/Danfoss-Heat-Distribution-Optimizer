\noindent
This chapter documents the work carried out during each of the project's
six sprints. For every sprint the goal, the team composition, the major
events of the Daily Standups, the outcome of the Sprint Review and the
conclusions drawn during the Sprint Retrospective are reported.

% =============================================================
\section{Sprint 1}
\label{sec:sprint1}
% =============================================================

The first sprint focused on establishing the ground of the project. The
sprint goal was \enquote{Create design tools for all of the modules and
establish the project infrastructure}, which consisted of the Noun--Verb
analysis, CRC cards and UML diagrams for each of the 5 modules (Asset
Manager, Source Data Manager, Result Data Manager, Optimizer and the
Data Visualizer). Alongside was established the project infrastructure
work: linking Jira with GitHub, creating the initial Avalonia project,
converting the XLSX file with data into a CSV file and storing it in the
assets folder.

Due to differences in experience and skillset levels inside the group,
the team was split into three pairs:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Maria Tsioni and Zoja Angelovicova
    \item Pavel Seliavko and Zoe Elea Zeilmann
    \item Dominik Bezo and Daniel Falat
\end{itemize}

Dominik Bezo acted as the Scrum Master, Pavel Seliavko acted as the
Meeting Coordinator and after discussion with the supervisor, Radoslaw
Marek Chylak, the role of the Product Owner was assigned to Radoslaw
Marek Chylak.

During the first Daily Standup it became clear there was space for
adding more tasks to the sprint. After consulting with the supervisor a
Figma prototype was added to the Sprint Backlog.

Throughout the Sprint Review, all of the design tools tickets had the
Noun--Verb analysis approved and marked as done, several CRC cards and
UML diagrams required further improvements, therefore these items were
moved back to the Product Backlog.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{sprint1-backlog-end.png}
    \caption{Screenshot of the backlog before closing the 1st sprint.}
    \label{fig:sprint1-backlog-end}
\end{figure}

The main conflicts were discussed during the Sprint Retrospective:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Dismissiveness and resistance to different ideas
    \item Inactivity, mainly during online meetings
    \item Lack of immediate feedback
    \item Underestimation of the workload
\end{itemize}

These issues were resolved by trying to adopt a more receptive approach,
agreeing on turning on cameras during online meetings, raising issues
immediately rather than waiting for the Sprint Retrospective and adding
a custom story point system (Difficulty 1--5 + Importance 1--5) to
better collaborate and estimate workload in future sprints.

% =============================================================
\section{Sprint 2}
\label{sec:sprint2}
% =============================================================

With a clearer picture of the workflow, and after improving certain
design tools, Sprint~2 focused on the actual implementation of the
first two modules: the Asset Manager and the Source Data Manager.
Building on the Figma prototype completed in Sprint~1, the first
version of the GUI was also built during this sprint. Each task was
further divided into smaller subtasks.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{sprint2-backlog-start.png}
    \caption{Screenshot of the backlog after starting the 2nd sprint.}
    \label{fig:sprint2-backlog-start}
\end{figure}

Pavel Seliavko obtained the role of the Scrum Master while also keeping
the role of the Meeting Coordinator. The original three pairs from
Sprint~1 remained unchanged and the agreed story point system was
applied to each ticket by each team (e.g.\ the Source Data Manager was
rated 5 for importance and 4 for difficulty).

Early in the sprint, during the first Daily Standup, it was recognised
that the initial plan -- which focused on the Data Visualizer -- was
not realistic, therefore the task was replaced with the implementation
of the Source Data Manager. This exchange showed that the initial
planning did not account for the full complexity of the whole
implementation.

The Sprint Retrospective concluded that one of the pairs assigned for
pair-programming struggled to find common working hours due to a
difference in time schedules, which slowed down the progression of
tickets assigned to this team. This issue was resolved by rotation of
the pair-programming partners in the following sprint, purposefully
pairing up teammates with more aligned schedules.

Sprint~2 successfully delivered the Asset Manager, the Source Data
Manager and the first version of the GUI. The initial burndown chart
appeared incorrect because Jira did not register the completed
subtasks until the whole tickets were moved to the \enquote{DONE}
category. This issue was realised at the very end of the sprint.

\section{Sprint 3}
\label{sec:sprint3}
% =============================================================

Sprint~3 focused on the remaining modules: the Optimizer, the Result
Data Manager and the Data Visualizer. Zoja Angelovicova acted as the
Scrum Master and Dominik Bezo as the Meeting Coordinator, while Pavel
Seliavko transitioned into the role of internal Product Owner. To
address the mid-sprint creation and replacement of tickets, a problem
identified in Sprint~2, the planning style was changed. Each pair
created its own detailed subtickets for the assigned module before
starting development. Because of the issue with the burndown chart from
Sprint~2, every subtask was created as a separate ticket so that Jira
would track progress correctly.

tickets remained outside the DONE column at the official end of the
sprint because of this. The conflicts were resolved by a full team
merge. All of the branches were merged into a single branch
\texttt{SCRUM-81-Version-1}. Furthermore several tickets had to be
renamed to reflect what had actually been implemented.

\section{Sprint 4}
\label{sec:sprint4}
% =============================================================

The main goal of Sprint~4 was to finalise and test the product. The
nature of the tasks aligned better with two teams of three group
members instead of the previous division into three pairs, which
matched the suggestion from the previous Sprint Retrospective:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Team Slovaks} -- Daniel Falat, Dominik Bezo and Zoja Angelovicova
    \item \textbf{Team International} -- Maria Tsioni, Pavel Seliavko and Zoe Elea Zeilmann
\end{itemize}

Daniel Falat took the Scrum Master role and Dominik Bezo took the role
of the Meeting Coordinator. Planned work included switching between
Scenario~1 and Scenario~2, refactoring of the Optimizer, removing
redundancy in the \texttt{TimeSeries}, implementing maintenance
selection, updating the Result Data Manager headers, applying the UML
feedback from the lecturer, and writing unit, functional and
integration tests for every module. Time estimation and the time
actually spent were also added to the tickets for the first time.

Because the focus was heavily directed at making the technical product
fully working, the tickets regarding report-writing originally planned
for this sprint had to be returned to the backlog at the end of the
sprint.

    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{sprint4-completed-2.png}
    \caption{Completed work items from Sprint 4.}
    \label{fig:sprint4-completed}

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering

The main conflict of the sprint surfaced during the Task Review: the
two teams had independently produced very similar solutions for
different tasks (SCRUM-117 \& 118 and SCRUM-128 \& 131), creating
duplicate code. The conflict was resolved through a discussion among
the whole team where it was decided that, for documentation reasons,
the best resolution was to keep the duplicate implementation while
only keeping one solution active. By the Sprint Review on the 1st of
May the Minimum Viable Product was complete and verified through
testing. Report writing had to be transferred to Sprint~5. The team
agreed that a heavyweight \textsc{Start} / \textsc{Stop} /
\textsc{Continue} retrospective was unnecessary this sprint because
collaboration inside both teams had worked well.

\section{Sprint 5}
\label{sec:sprint5}
% =============================================================

Sprint~5 focused mainly on the division of the report tasks,
report-writing and refactoring of the code. Because of the
Math~2 exam on the 8th of May, a thorough Sprint Planning meeting was
held before that on the 3rd of May. The sprint was formally started on
the 9th of May, with the end date moved to the 17th of May. Maria
became Scrum Master and Zoja the Meeting Coordinator, while Pavel
remained the Product Owner. The two teams from Sprint~4 were kept.

On the code side, Team Slovaks took the task of making the Data
Visualizer call the Optimizer only once with a clearer separation of
concerns, and Team International took the refactoring of the Unit
models to use more abstraction/inheritance and fewer interfaces. For
the report, every chapter was assigned to an individual writer and
reviewer (see Appendix), except for Chapter~5 (Implementation), which
is co-written by both teams. A dedicated meeting was scheduled for the
16th of May to produce the final Class, Activity and Sequence diagrams
used in the report.

\begin{figure}[h!]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{sprint5-backlog-start.png}
    \caption{Screenshot of the backlog after starting the 5th sprint.}
    \label{fig:sprint5-backlog-start}
\end{figure}

During the Sprint Review and Retrospective, no major issues were
identified, but the report-writing tickets had to be moved again to the
next sprint, with the tickets already being in progress.
